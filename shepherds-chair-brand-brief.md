### About the Owners

**Nathan Mercer** (42) and **Joel Mercer** (38) are brothers born and raised in Northeast Portland. Both attended David Douglas High School and grew up in a working-class family in the Parkrose neighborhood. Their father ran a small auto repair shop on Sandy Boulevard — they grew up watching him build something from nothing with his hands, which shaped everything about how they do business.

Both brothers are members of **Imago Dei Community Church** (SE Portland) — a culturally engaged, non-denominational evangelical congregation known for its emphasis on creativity, justice, and neighborhood rootedness. Their faith is present but never performative. It shows up in how they treat their staff, how they engage the community, and what they refuse to do — they don't open on Sundays, they pay above minimum wage across all units, and they donate a percentage of monthly revenue to **JOIN PDX**, a local organization focused on ending homelessness.

Nathan handles operations, growth, and partnerships. Joel is the creative mind — he designed the original shop layout himself and still occasionally works behind a chair on Saturdays. He describes himself as "a barber who learned to be a businessman, not the other way around."

The name *The Shepherd's Chair* was Joel's idea, arrived at during a conversation with their pastor after the first year of business. He wanted a name that carried weight — something that pointed, quietly, to the idea that good care is a calling, not a transaction. A shepherd tends to each one. The chair is where that happens. Nathan said yes immediately.

They chose not to explain the name publicly. It's on the sign. People who get it, get it.

> *"We wanted the guy who works at Powell's to feel as comfortable here as the guy who works at Intel. Same chair, same quality, same conversation."*
> — Joel Mercer

### Client's Brief Summary *(from discovery interview)*

- Build a website that feels like walking into the shop: **warm, intentional, unhurried**
- It should communicate **craft and community** above everything else
- Avoid anything that feels like a chain or a tech startup
- They want **real faces, real names** — the team is the product
- The site should drive **online booking** and **franchise inquiries**

## 4. Brand Identity

### 4.1 Brand Name & Concept
**The Shepherd's Chair**

The name carries two registers simultaneously, and that's the whole point.

On the surface: a chair is the center of a barbershop. The shepherd tends — carefully, individually, without rushing — each one under his care. It's an image of craft and attention. Of someone who knows the difference between customer 47 and *this person, right now, in front of me.*

Underneath: the pastoral reference is unmistakably biblical for those who know it. The 23rd Psalm. The good shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine. The idea that care is a calling. Nathan and Joel never say this in their marketing. They don't need to. The name holds it.

The result is a brand that speaks to everyone — and says something extra to some.

---

### 4.2 Logo Concept — Visual Description for AI Generation

**Style:** Refined artisan crest. The visual language of a guild mark — something that looks like it was designed with the understanding that it would need to hold up on a brass door plate, an embossed leather apron patch, and a website favicon. Restraint is the principle. Every element earns its place.

**Core shape:** A vertical oval or shield crest — slightly elongated, with clean geometric framing. Fine double-rule border. Feels like a seal, not a sticker.

**Central element:** A shepherd's crook — rendered as a clean, single-weight line illustration. Elegant, not folksy. The crook curves naturally and its staff extends downward, where it subtly transforms into the silhouette of a straight razor handle — the curve of the hook at top, the blade implied at the base. The two tools (crook and razor) share one continuous line. This is the key visual idea: *one motion, two meanings.*

**Typography within the mark:**
- "THE SHEPHERD'S CHAIR" in refined, tight all-caps serif — occupying the top arc of the crest, wide letter-spacing
- "EST. 2017" centered at the base, small, in a fine sans-serif
- "PORTLAND · OR" on the lower arc, smaller still — almost whispered

**Color (logo):**
- Primary: Deep forest ink (`#1C2B1E`) on warm parchment (`#F4EFE4`)
- Secondary: Aged gold (`#B49A3D`) on the dark ink — for merchandise, dark backgrounds, embossing
- Monochrome: Single-ink for stamps, apron patches, wax seals

**Feel:** Like the mark on the door of something that has been good at what it does for a long time. Timeless without being nostalgic. Serious without being cold.

### 4.4 Typography — Google Fonts

#### Display / Hero Headlines
**[Cormorant Garamond](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Cormorant+Garamond)**
- Weights: 600 (SemiBold), 700 (Bold)
- Usage: Large hero text, section titles, pull quotes
- Character: Old-world elegance with optical refinement. Literary, serious, beautiful. Carries the weight the name requires without being heavy.

#### Section Headings
**[DM Serif Display](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/DM+Serif+Display)**
- Weights: 400 (Regular — this font's weight is already punchy)
- Usage: H2, H3, subheadings throughout
- Character: Editorial and confident. Pairs with Cormorant without competing.

#### Body / UI Text
**[Plus Jakarta Sans](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Plus+Jakarta+Sans)**
- Weights: 400 (Regular), 500 (Medium), 600 (SemiBold)
- Usage: All body copy, navigation, buttons, labels, meta text
- Character: Warm humanist sans-serif. Avoids cold sterility. Feels considered and grounded.

#### Accent / Taglines / Quotes
**[Lora](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Lora)**
- Weights: 400 Italic
- Usage: Pull quotes, testimonials, taglines, small accent labels
- Character: Refined and literary in italic. Used sparingly — it's the pause, the breath between statements.

#### Type Scale (Desktop)
```
Hero:          Cormorant Garamond 700 — 80–96px / line-height 1.05
H1:            Cormorant Garamond 600 — 56–72px / line-height 1.1
H2:            DM Serif Display 400 — 40–48px / line-height 1.2
H3:            DM Serif Display 400 — 28–32px / line-height 1.3
Body Large:    Plus Jakarta Sans 400 — 18px / line-height 1.7
Body:          Plus Jakarta Sans 400 — 16px / line-height 1.75
```

## 6. The Shop — Space Description

### Exterior Description *(Alberta Street Flagship)*

The Alberta Street location occupies a 1920s brick storefront — the kind of building that has held at least four different small businesses over the decades and wears the evidence with dignity. The brick is original, painted in deep matte forest green with the wordmark *The Shepherd's Chair* in aged gold letterpress above the door — the font is the same display serif used across all brand materials, cut cleanly into a dark wood plaque mounted flush against the brick.

A wide single-pane window faces the street — passersby can see directly into the shop. The window has a small vinyl shepherd's crook mark in the lower corner and gold lettered hours. A wooden A-frame sign sits on the sidewalk — blackboard face with the week's featured service hand-lettered in white chalk.

Two large terracotta planters flank the entrance — kept clean and seasonally planted (currently: trailing rosemary and a small olive tree). The door is original wood, dark stained, with a brass pull handle and a small handwritten card in the window reading: *"The chair is ready."* When fully booked for walk-ins, the card reads: *"Come back tomorrow. We'll be here."*

The overall feeling: a shop that has been there forever, even though it hasn't. That's the intention.

---

### Interior Description *(Alberta Street Flagship)*

**Layout:** Three barber chairs in a single row facing a full-length mirror wall. Chairs are vintage-style hydraulic — dark tobacco leather, brushed brass base, wide armrests. Not reproduction antiques; genuine classic Koken chairs, restored and reupholstered in a warm cognac-brown. Behind each chair: individual floating dark walnut shelves with that barber's personal tools and products, organized with the precision of a craftsperson who knows where everything is without looking.

**Floors:** Original hardwood — deep walnut tone, refinished but not perfectly so. Character is preserved. A narrow runner of dark green wool sits between the chairs and the waiting area.

**Walls:** Left wall: exposed brick (original building material). Right wall: deep forest green — matte, warm, rich. One oversized framed vintage map of Portland — NE quadrant, dated 1954. Below it, a shelf of books: local Oregon history, photography books, *Barbering: The Art and Science* (the trade textbook, worn), and a rotating selection from Powell's — Joel picks them.

**Lighting:** Three large cage-style pendant lights hang above each station — Edison bulbs, amber. Track lighting above the product shelf. A small brass banker's lamp at Elena's front desk.

**Front desk area:** A reclaimed dark wood counter with a simple point-of-sale setup, a small glass jar of peppermints, and a card rack displaying work by local Alberta Arts District artists — for sale, rotating quarterly. Behind the desk, curated retail: 8–10 products, spaced generously on a floating shelf — no visual clutter.

**Waiting area:** Two low leather bench seats with hairpin legs — cognac leather, vintage-inspired. A small side table with current issues of *Portland Monthly*, *The Raven*, and one rotating independent magazine. A Bluetooth speaker plays — always — a Joel-curated playlist. When asked, he says it's "the soundtrack a barbershop would play if it had actually good taste."

**Scent:** Coffee from the drip machine near the back. Something faint and clean — a cedar and vetiver reed diffuser near the entrance. The smell of good product — no artificial fragrance.

**The Detail That People Notice:**
On the wall between the second and third chair, a small framed calligraphy print — simple cream ink on deep green — reads:

> *"A man who is good at his work is good for something."*
> — Proverbs 22:29

No cross. No church name. No announcement. Just the words, in a good frame, at eye level.

## 7. Photography Style

### Overall Direction
**Aesthetic:** Warm editorial documentary. Think: a mid-career photographer who shoots for *Kinfolk* magazine but grew up reading *LIFE*. Nothing staged that looks staged. Nothing candid that looks sloppy.

### Lighting
- **Primary:** Natural window light from the Alberta Street storefront — soft, directional, golden. Shoot during the 2–4pm window when light rakes across the chairs from the west-facing window.
- **Secondary:** Practical lighting from the pendant fixtures — adds warmth and depth without becoming the primary source
- **Never:** Flash photography. Ring lights. Studio lighting that removes depth or flattens the environment.

### Color Treatment
- Warm, slightly desaturated — colors present but not oversaturated
- Skin tones take priority in every edit — no filtration that flattens melanin or shifts tone
- Shadow detail preserved — the shop has intentional shadow and it should read on camera
- Overall tone: like a photograph taken on film and digitized with care. Green tones should feel *forest*, not neon.

### Composition & Framing

**Portrait style (team shots):**
- Medium format feel — 50mm equivalent, slight depth of field
- Subject at work or in natural pose — never staring at camera mid-action, but direct eye contact *or* three-quarter profiles both work
- Environmental portraits: barber at their station, hands visible, tools in background
- Square format for Instagram / portrait crop for web

**Detail shots:**
- Extreme close-up: razor on leather strop, comb against scalp, fingers in hair during a cut
- Shallow depth of field — foreground tool sharp, everything else impressionistic
- Product shots: on the shelf, in hand, never on white backgrounds

**Shop/space shots:**
- Wide establishing shot: full interior from entrance angle
- Architectural detail: brick wall texture, the framed Proverbs print, the chairs reflected in the mirror, the green exterior and A-frame on the sidewalk
- Light studies: window light on an empty chair mid-morning, before the first appointment

**Action/service shots:**
- Barber and client in conversation — not posed, captured
- The moment of concentration: barber's eyes focused, client relaxed
- The reveal: client seeing the finished cut — that half-second of recognition

### What These Images Should Feel Like
*Worn leather. Good coffee. A pew with better lighting.*

## 8. Communication Identity & Voice

### Brand Voice
The voice of The Shepherd's Chair is **the craft-confident neighbor**. They know what they're doing. They don't need to announce it. They make you feel at home — not like a consumer, not like a project, not like an appointment slot.

**Tone attributes:**
- Warm, not saccharine
- Confident, not arrogant
- Grounded, not corporate
- Occasionally dry — PNW-specific humor that earns the laugh
- Honest — will tell you the truth about what works

**What the voice is NOT:**
- Hustle culture / "grind" language
- Luxury / exclusivity signaling
- Preachy or moralistic — faith shows in *action*, never in copy
- Trendy slang that dates in 18 months
- Corporate-casual ("We're passionate about your journey...")

### Subtle Faith Language
*(Present in tone and action — never announced in copy)*

- The name itself carries the pastoral reference for those who recognize it — and works as a beautiful image of craft and care for those who don't
- The Proverbs print in the shop — visible in photography, never captioned
- "Closed Sundays" — stated plainly in the footer and location cards. No explanation offered. No asterisk.
- The JOIN PDX partnership — framed not as charity, but as *being a good neighbor*
- The value *"Integrity in the Quiet Things"* — ethics-forward language that speaks without preaching
- The phrase *"a standard of care"* is used deliberately throughout — care as a calling, not a service feature
